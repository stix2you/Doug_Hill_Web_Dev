My Web Development Journey

Career Foundry

After two rewarding careers in Auto Mechanics and Pro Audio Sales, I have decided to return to one of my first loves: computers. If I'm honest, that was where I spent most of the time when I was a kid. Having grown up in the 80's, learning Basic on an Apple //e, then C++ in the 90's on PC based machines, computers have always been a hobby, much like auto repair always was from the moment I could pick up a wrench.

I didn't come to the auto repair world without a solid foundation for mechanics, and it's a similar story with Web Development. When I was younger, I was always the one pulling apart the games and programs I was using, trying to figure out how they worked on a software side. I spent a great deal of time helping my friend with her LAN, which she installed in her house in the 90's in order to practice for her day job doing IT at an accounting firm. As a teenager, I spent many hours coding in Basic and trying to get programs to work, and hung around with like minded friends who were also into computers.

This is why I know my skills as an Auto Mechanic will translate well into Web Development. In Mechanics I was always drawn to the electrical and computer side of modern vehicles, and I began to specialize in advanced vehicle diagnostics, using oscilloscopes and other tools to trace signals in and out of the computers in the vehicles. I was always the one who was called upon to diagnose the most difficult problems, and I was always the one who was able to solve them. I guess it's no surprise that the computer side of vehicles spoke to me so strongly, as they are essentially several different computers linked together with three to five networks (Including an optical network in many cars now).

After some careful research on bootcamps, I came across Career Foundry. I have been impressed with how much they push their students to learn, and how much they expect from them. It speaks to my desire to push myself to learn as much as I can, and to be the best I can be.

Auto Mechanics

For about the past 15 years I have been an active auto mechanic. I trained at Madison College (Madison, Wisconsin) from 2007 to 2009, and dove in the deep end by embracing "A" level auto repair work and advanced diagnostics from the very beginning of my professional career.

The mechanical part of the work was not as challenging, and so as I progressed and grew within the field, I found myself drawn to the electrical and computer side of modern vehicles. I began to specialize in advanced electrical and computer diagnosics. At Foreign Car Specialists, we would receive vehicles which even the dealership had given up on, and tackled the computer module replacements, wiring harness repairs, vehicle network diagnostics, vehicle programming, and diagnostics which involved combining the mechanical world with the electrical world. When interfacing between the two, I often found myself with an oscilloscope in hand looking directly at signal going in and out of the computers in order to fault trace. Some of my favorite diagnostics involved the use of pressure transducers, to scope a pattern which showed exactly how an individual cylinder was moving air in and out of it.

As time went on, the workflow at the shop became problematic to track, and so I developed a tracking system and a parts inventory system using just spreadsheets and Quickbooks. But it was every bit as effective as costly management software. That project in itself showed me I could benefit from getting into API's and databases, to be able to design a more efficient system and one that could be customer tailored to a shop. The challenges of managing the business were attractive to me, beyond the challenges of fixing cars. And I enjoyed developing computer based solutions to manage the workflow, parts integration into the workflow, inventory control, and communications.

I continue to work on cars to make money on the side, and I still enjoy mechanics.

Pro Audio and Music

My first career began in the 90's, after receiving my Bachelor's degree in Music. During the day I worked selling Pro Audio Equipment Sales, working for a nationwide company called Full Compass Systems. Nights and weekends found me playing as a drummer and percussionist in several bands and recording projects. After college, I spent about 10 years completely immersed in this world before deciding to make a change to better support my family.

During this time however, again I found I gravitated to the computer side of the business. My last six months at Full Compass Systems found me managing web sales. Back in 2004, the web was still a new frontier for sales, and so we did little more than sell on eBay and Amazon. But it did give me insight into the potential of the web for sales, and I began to see the potential for the web to be a powerful tool for business. I also developed the workflow for web sales, and managed that along with customer communications.

Captioning for the Hearing Impaired

In an effort to supplement my income, for about 10 years I worked for a company who produced a phone which provided real time captions to their clients, directly on the telephone. It was part time, and I was mostly a captionist, using programs like ViaVoice and Dragon to produce captions along with a keyboard and macros.

However, I was often pulled off the phones to assist in training and quality control. I was one of several people who helped to transition captionists from training to on-the-floor captioning, which took about a week of sit-behind monitoring and coaching. I also helped to monitor calls and provide feedback to captionists, in order to improve captions. Working closely with managers and the training team over the course of the 10 years I was there, I learned a great deal about managing and training people.
